Gender

In French, every noun has either a natural gender, for instance le homme (man) is masculine, or a grammatical gender. Grammatical gender is a gender assigned to the noun, and is not as intuitive like natural gender. For instance in English, ships are referred to with a she.


The same concept applies in French, except on a much wider scale, because every noun has its own gender and unless it is words such as la femme (woman) that have a natural gender, it is necessary to remember the gender along with the word.


This is not as difficult a task as it may seem though, because the French word for a and the varies based on the gender of a noun.


Masc. Sing.Fem. Sing.Plural
aununedes
thelelales

Hence, because the french word for game jeu is masculine, the game would be le jeu, a game would be un jeu, and when revising vocab you would learn un jeu NOT just jeu.


But, because the french word for mug tasse is feminine, the game would be la tasse, a game would be une tasse, and when revising vocab you would learn une tasse NOT just tasse.


Remember that it is only the singular forms of a noun, that inflect (change) based upon gender. Also you may wonder what is the plural of a in English, since the plural in French is des. In English, des translates to some. Note however, that whilst in English neither some nor the is required when a noun is in its plural form, they are required in French. So you can never have la femme on its own in neither the plural nor the singular.
